Opening the Bones

The Necromancy of an Axiom

We begin before we begin
‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ

You are standing at the mouth of something ancient‚Äîmaybe myth, maybe memory, maybe both. This is a story you already know, carried in bruised childhood sayings, hollow silences, and dreams you didn't know how to remember. Here, we do not rush. There is no timer. No test. No need to perform the shape of healing. You do not need to believe anything yet. You do not even need to agree. All that is needed now is a slight leaning forward, a tilt of the soul toward something different‚Äîsomething alive.

"Sticks and stones may break my bones, but words will never hurt me."

This was never a shield. It was a muzzle. A survival phrase born in a time where silence was safer than honesty. Whispered to children across generations, taught in schoolyards, repeated in moments of pain‚Äîthis simple phrase has become embedded in our collective consciousness. It appears deceptively ancient, as if carved into the stones of our earliest villages, chanted around primal fires when our ancestors first gathered in tribes. Yet this saying is surprisingly young, emerging not from the dawn of humanity but from more recent centuries, likely taking its modern form during the 19th century. This relative youth speaks volumes about how quickly we can normalize and internalize ideas‚Äîeven harmful ones‚Äîwhen they serve a perceived need.

If these words have ever been used to invalidate your pain or justify someone else's harm, you are not alone. They've been weaponized, recycled, etched into cartoons, classrooms, courtrooms. For many of us, including myself, it became a necessary tool for survival, a way to move through hostile environments without shattering under their weight.

Yet this shield, for all its immediate comfort, came with hidden costs. It taught us to deny the real impact of verbal and emotional harm. It silently normalized abuse by framing it as inconsequential. It disconnected us from our authentic responses to cruelty. Most insidiously, it placed the burden of resilience on the wounded rather than responsibility on those inflicting wounds.

Let this be said clearly: What happened to you was not okay. Teaching children to deny the impact of emotional harm is not okay. Creating a culture where verbal violence is normalized is not okay. Placing the burden of resilience on the wounded rather than responsibility on those causing harm is not okay.

Consider the deeper implications: A society that teaches its most vulnerable members that words cannot hurt them is also teaching that verbal violence is acceptable. That emotional harm is not "real" harm. That the appropriate response to abuse is to internally deny its impact rather than to confront its source. This is not just a shield but a silencing‚Äîa way to maintain power structures by training the powerless to accept their condition.

This phenomenon reminds us of Shirley Jackson's "The Lottery"‚Äîpractices continued without question, generation after generation, even as they cause profound harm. We repeat the phrase without examining what it truly asks of us, or whose interests it ultimately serves. The axiom has become a kind of necromancy‚Äîa reanimated corpse of wisdom that shambles on long after its spirit has fled.

Modern neuroscience, psychology, and simply living attentively in human bodies have confirmed what many always intuitively knew: words wound. They alter neural pathways. They change hormone production. They reshape our internal narratives. They can inflict wounds more lasting than many physical injuries. To deny this reality is not resilience but a kind of enforced dissociation‚Äîa fracturing of our connection to our own experience.

And yet‚Äîthere is wisdom hidden within this flawed shield. It points toward something true: that we need ways to navigate harm, to find agency in difficult circumstances, to carry ourselves with dignity through pain. The problem is not that it seeks resilience, but that it seeks it through denial rather than integration. Through silence rather than voice. Through isolation rather than connection.

What if we could preserve the strength while releasing the silence? What if we could acknowledge harm while claiming agency? What if we could transform this brittle shield into something living, something that protects without isolating, that empowers without denying?

If you are still reading, that is enough. Your presence here matters. Your witness matters.

This is not just a philosophical question but a practical one. The words we whisper to children become the narratives adults live by. The shields we offer shape not just individual psyches but collective cultures. If we continue passing this axiom unchanged, we perpetuate its harms. If we simply discard it without replacement, we leave the vulnerable unequipped for the realities they face.

We stand at a threshold of possibility. We can honor what this axiom attempted while transforming what it achieved. We can recognize its historical function while refusing to be bound by its limitations. We can acknowledge the real protection it offered while creating something more whole, more true, more alive to replace it.

A new axiom waits to be born‚Äînot from denial but from integration. Not from silencing but from voice. Not from isolation but from connection. In the pages that follow, we will explore this transformation, this rebirth, this reclaiming of our relationship with words, with harm, with protection, and with each other.

The journey begins with recognition: Words have power. They always have. And what we do with that power‚Äîhow we wield it, how we respond to it, how we transform it‚Äîshapes not just personal resilience but the very nature of our shared world.

Healing waits at the edge of atrocity. It will not walk in while the fire still burns. Now, let us open the bones together.
